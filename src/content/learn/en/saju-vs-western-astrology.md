Ask a Korean fortune teller and a Western astrologer the same question — *"what does my birth say about me?"* — and both will ask for the same three facts: date, time, and place of birth. From there, the two systems part ways completely.

## What each system actually reads

**Western astrology** maps the sky. It records where the sun, moon, and planets sat against the zodiac at your birth moment, then divides the sky into twelve houses anchored to your birthplace's horizon. The chart is a snapshot of *space*.

**Saju (사주, Four Pillars)** maps the calendar. It encodes your birth year, month, day, and hour into eight characters drawn from a repeating 60-unit cycle of stems and branches. There are no planets in a saju chart at all — the "astronomy" lives in how the calendar itself is defined, with months beginning at solar terms (the sun crossing every 15° of ecliptic longitude). The chart is a snapshot of *time*.

## Quick comparison

| | Saju | Western astrology |
|---|---|---|
| Core unit | 8 characters (four pillars) | Planets in signs and houses |
| "You" in the chart | The day master (day stem) | The sun sign, ascendant |
| Personality model | Balance of five elements | Element + modality of placements |
| Timing | 10-year luck cycles (daeun), yearly and monthly cycles | Transits, progressions |
| Birthplace's role | Converts clock time to true local time | Sets houses and ascendant |
| Year boundary | Ipchun (start of spring, ~Feb 4) | Not a chart concept |

## Three differences that surprise people

**1. The year doesn't change on January 1.** In saju, the year pillar flips at *ipchun*, the solar term near February 4. If you were born in mid-January, your saju year animal is the *previous* year's — which regularly surprises people comparing their "zodiac animal" across systems.

**2. There's no "moon sign."** The moon, so central to Western charts, plays no direct role in the four pillars. Saju's lunar connection is only at the input step: Koreans historically recorded birthdays in the lunisolar calendar, which is converted to a solar date before the chart is cast.

**3. The reference point is you, not the sky.** A Western chart reads meaning from planetary relationships. A saju chart reads every character *relative to your day master* — the same character is auspicious in one person's chart and challenging in another's. That relational grammar (the "ten gods") is what makes two charts with similar elements read very differently.

## Where they rhyme

Both are, at heart, element-balance systems (five elements in saju, four in astrology), both layer a timing system over a natal snapshot, and both — at their best — are used less for prediction than for structured self-reflection. Korean practitioners often describe saju as a *weather report for your life*: it doesn't decide where you sail, it describes the winds.
